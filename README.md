# omidvheravi.github.io

An algorithmic philosophy for the Berlekamp–van Lint–Seidel graph
Perfect Residue is a generative aesthetic movement devoted to a single object: the strongly regular graph SRG(243, 22, 1, 2), discovered in 1973 inside the shadow of the perfect ternary Golay code. The movement holds that the most beautiful structures in mathematics are residues — what remains when a perfect object is quotiented away. The 243 vertices of this graph are not arbitrary points; they are the cosets of the [11, 6, 5]₃ Golay code, the equivalence classes left behind by perfection. Two residues touch when they differ by a single whispered symbol — one coordinate, one nonzero value, out of eleven. The movement insists this arithmetic be real: the algorithm must construct the graph honestly, from the generator polynomial x⁵ + 2x³ + x² + 2x + 2 dividing x¹¹ − 1 over GF(3), through syndrome to Cayley connection set, never approximated, never faked. Computational truth is the canvas primer.
The aesthetic core is structured saturation. With 2,673 edges among 243 vertices, every drawing of this graph threatens to collapse into noise — and yet strong regularity guarantees it cannot: every vertex sees 22 others, every edge lives in exactly one triangle, every non-edge is bridged by exactly two paths. The philosophy demands that rendering parameters be tuned with painstaking care so that this hidden discipline bleeds through the density — translucent threads accumulating into moiré whorls, interference fringes, and caustic arcs that no random graph could produce. The viewer should feel order pressing against chaos like light through woven silk. This is controlled saturation refined through countless iterations, the mark of a meticulously crafted algorithm rather than a plotted dataset.
Color in Perfect Residue is provenance, not decoration. Every edge of the Cayley graph descends from one of the eleven coordinates of the Golay code — the position whose weight-one error generated its syndrome. The palette is therefore an eleven-step gradient, and each thread carries the hue of its ancestral coordinate. What looks like a freely painted nebula is in fact a complete genealogy of error patterns. The conceptual seed is buried exactly here: eleven hues for eleven coordinates, three-fold arithmetic in every position, the ghost of the perfect code visible only to those who know to count.
Form follows the group. The vertex set is Z₃⁵, and the movement renders it through three projections, each a different act of attention. The Chord projection places all 243 residues on a circle ordered by their base-3 expansion under a seeded automorphism of coordinates and symbols — Bezier threads pulled toward the center reveal the translation symmetry as rippling interference. The Shells projection arranges vertices in concentric rings by trit-weight (1, 10, 40, 80, 80, 32 — the Pascal triangle of GF(3) made flesh). The Strata projection stacks eleven rows by digit sum, a lantern of layered light echoing the graph's two-distance geometry: from any vertex, 22 neighbors, 220 strangers, none farther. Seeded randomness never invents structure; it only chooses which automorphic mask the structure wears — a permutation of the five coordinates, a relabeling of the three symbols, a breath of radial jitter.
Time is the final medium. The graph must not appear; it must accumulate. Edges resolve a few dozen per frame, threads settling like sediment in still water, while a slow rotation lets the chord-whorls precess. The piece reaches equilibrium the way the code reaches perfection — inevitably, with nothing left over. Every parameter exposed to the viewer (saturation of threads, pull of the bundling, speed of the sediment, breath of the jitter) was calibrated by a master of computational aesthetics so that any setting yields a coherent image and the defaults yield a transcendent one. The same seed must always reproduce the same residue, exactly, forever — reproducibility is the movement's signature of craftsmanship, the proof that beauty here is not an accident but a theorem.
